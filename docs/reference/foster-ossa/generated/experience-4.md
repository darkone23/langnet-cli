## toc:4.7 - gerund and gerundive

Source refs: `page:529`, `page:530`, `page:531`, `page:532`, `page:533`

### Method Claims
- A gerund is a verbal singular neuter noun.
- Gerunds and gerundives have no difference in their verbal meaning; only their forms are different; one is a noun, the other an adjective.
- The ancient Romans prefer the gerundive by 95% and use the gerund only 5% of the time with an object.
- The rule for forming a gerundive from a gerund is: Put the object of the gerund in the function of the gerund and make the gerundive agree with it.
- The distinction between the participle of passive necessity and the gerund/gerundive is so difficult that they are presented in two separate Experiences, separated by one full academic year.

### Learner Actions
- Transform gerund constructions into gerundive constructions by putting the object in the gerund's function and making the gerundive agree.
- Identify the gerund as a noun and the gerundive as an adjective.
- Recognize and use the four functions: subject, object, of-possession, by-with-from-in, to-for-from.
- Distinguish gerund/gerundive from the participle of passive necessity by meaning (active vs. passive).

## toc:4.19 - Passiva formula obliquis cum verbis / the passive expression of verbs with genitive, dative, ablative

Source refs: `page:580`, `page:581`, `page:582`, `page:583`, `page:584`, `page:585`, `page:586`, `page:587`

### Method Claims
- The language is presented in dictionary entries and examples, not from any sort of grammar book.
- Intransitive verbs with oblique complements form their passive impersonally: the active verb becomes impersonal, the active subject becomes an agent, and the oblique complement remains unchanged.

- Form the passive of an intransitive verb by making the verb impersonal, expressing the active subject as agent, and leaving the oblique complement untouched.
- Recognize that an English transitive rendering (e.g., 'the students will be assisted by me') is not a direct translation of the Latin impersonal passive.
- Apply the same impersonal passive rules to gerunds of intransitive verbs: keep the oblique complement and avoid turning it into a gerundive.
