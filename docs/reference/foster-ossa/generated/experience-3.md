## toc:3.1 - Adiectivorum comparatio et varia significatio

Source refs: `page:301`, `page:302`, `page:303`, `page:304`, `page:305`

### Method Claims
- The comparative degree for all adjectives follows one pattern: masculine and feminine end in –ior, neuter ends in –ius.
- The superlative degree for all adjectives follows the pattern of Bk.I nouns and adjectives (ending in us, a, um).
- The comparative is declined like a Bk.II noun (e.g., imperator).
- The superlative is declined like a Bk.I noun and adjective.
- No memorization of declensions or foolishness is required.
- These two rules cover 90% of adjectives.
- The comparative degree has special meanings: 'more beautiful,' 'somewhat beautiful,' 'too beautiful,' 'quite beautiful,' 'rather beautiful.'
- The superlative degree may be absolute ('very beautiful') or relative ('most beautiful').
- A few irregular forms exist and are listed from Gildersleeve and Lodge.

### Learner Actions
- Treat the comparative adjective like a Bk.II noun (imperator, senator).
- Treat the superlative adjective like a Bk.I noun and adjective (us, a, um).
- Form the comparative plural by analogy with imperatorum, corporum.
- Form the neuter plural comparative by taking the second entry in the dictionary (e.g., honestioris → honestiora).
- Use the absolute superlative ('very happy') when the relative does not fit.
- Watch for neuter comparative forms like honestius, altiora that may be mistaken for masculine or feminine.
- Consult the dictionary for comparative and superlative under the positive adjective entry.
- Find real Latin texts containing these items.
