## toc:1.5 - Adiectiva Velut Substantiva; Brevitas Et Ambiguitas / adjectives used as nouns according to their gender; brevity and ambiguity

### Method Claims
- This is very popular and extremely frequent in Latin.
- The use of an adjective as a noun is extremely important, especially in the neuter.
- Because in so many instances throughout all of Latin literature the noun is not expressed, but is thus contained in the adjective, you are going to have extremely pithy, short, and effective Latin sentences that use this phenomenon.

## toc:1.8 - Principal parts of verbs. Times 4, 5, 6 of all verbs: easy construction

Source refs: `page:79`, `page:80`, `page:81`, `page:82`

### Method Claims
- All forms of Times 4, 5, 6 in active direct discourse and question can be derived from the third principal part without memorizing conjugations.
- For Time 4, take the third principal part and use the personal endings from Day 1 (including special endings -sti for 'you' and -stis for 'you all').
- For Time 5, replace the final -i of the third principal part with -eram.
- For Time 6, replace the final -i of the third principal part with -ero (noting that final o can shift).
- The fourth principal part is common but not present for all verbs; it is often called the perfect passive participle.

### Learner Actions
- Consult the dictionary for the four principal parts of any verb.
- Form Time 4 by attaching personal endings to the third principal part (e.g., vidi -> vidisti, vidit, vidimus, vidistis, viderunt or videre).
- Form Time 5 by changing the final -i of the third principal part to -eram and then adding personal endings (e.g., veni -> veneram, veneras, venerat...).
- Form Time 6 by changing the final -i of the third principal part to -ero and then adding personal endings, being careful with vowel changes (e.g., veni -> venero, veneris, venerit...).
- Practice by finding examples on reading sheets rather than writing out conjugation charts.

## toc:1.10 - Pronomen relativum: subiectum + obiectum. Quattuor principia / the relative pronoun as subject and object. four principles

Source refs: `page:86`, `page:87`, `page:88`

### Method Claims
- The rule for choosing or understanding the relative pronoun is that its gender and number come from outside its own clause, but its function comes from inside its own clause.
- The antecedent gives only its gender and number to the relative pronoun; the antecedent's function does not influence the relative pronoun's form or function.

### Learner Actions
- Determine whether the relative pronoun is masculine, feminine, or neuter.
- Determine whether it is singular or plural.
- Identify the relative pronoun's function (subject, object, etc.) inside its own clause.
- Find the antecedent outside the relative clause, expressed or understood.
- Use the verb of the relative clause to help decide whether the relative pronoun is singular or plural.
